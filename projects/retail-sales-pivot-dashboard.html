View dashboard →

Problem statement

Retail businesses generate thousands of transaction-level records that, on their own, don't drive decisions. Leadership needs a self-service view that answers a handful of recurring questions: which region is most profitable, when demand peaks, whether discounting is helping or hurting margin, and which product lines are worth keeping, without waiting on an analyst to pull a new report every time. This project builds that view using the Superstore dataset from Kaggle, a synthetic retail transactions dataset rather than data from a real business.

Tools used

  • Microsoft Excel for Pivot Tables, PivotCharts, Slicers (with Report Connections), Conditional Formatting, and formulas including TRIM(), PROPER(), DATEVALUE(), ISNUMBER(), nested IF(), and ROUNDUP().

Process

  1. Standardized inconsistent text casing and whitespace in the Category, Region, and Segment fields, and normalized inconsistent date formats, before removing duplicates, so formatting differences wouldn't mask true duplicate rows. Removed roughly 25 exact duplicate rows, corrected 3 discount values that were off by a factor of 10 (decimal-point entry errors), and fixed 6 negative Quantity values (sign errors). Flagged and excluded 4 rows with an unresolvable Sales = 0 but Profit ≠ 0 inconsistency rather than fabricating a Sales value, and left missing values in non-KPI fields (Customer Name, Postal Code, Product Name) untouched since they don't feed into any of the dashboard's metrics.
  2. Engineered two new fields: a Profit Margin column (Profit ÷ Sales) and a Discount Bucket column (0%, 1 to 20%, 21 to 40%, 40%+), to support two of the five KPIs.
  3. Built five pivot tables, one per business question, each turned into a chart matched to the data: bar charts for category comparisons, a line chart for trends, and combo charts with a secondary axis where a dollar amount and a ratio needed to appear together.
  4. Combined all five charts and two headline KPI cards (Total Sales, Total Profit) onto one Dashboard sheet, with Region and Segment slicers connected to every chart simultaneously for self-service filtering.

Key findings

  • Total sales and profit: $2,297,434 in sales and $286,568 in profit, about a 12.5% overall margin.
  • Most profitable region: West, both in total profit and profit margin.
  • Seasonal peak: November through December is a consistent sales peak across every year in the dataset, genuine seasonality rather than a one-time spike.
  • Discounting hurts margin: profit margin declines as discount level increases; heavier discounting is directly associated with thinner margins.
  • Unprofitable sub-categories: 5 sub-categories operate at a negative average profit margin despite generating real sales volume: Machines, Bookcases, Tables, Appliances, and Binders.
  • Year-over-year growth: steady growth in both sales and profit from 2014 to 2017.

Report

Superstore's overall profitability is healthy (about a 12.5% margin) but uneven across the business. The West region consistently outperforms the other three, making it a candidate for continued investment, while Central, Superstore's weakest region, warrants a closer look at pricing or cost structure. Sales reliably spike every November and December, so inventory and staffing plans should anticipate that seasonal surge rather than react to it. Discounting comes at a real cost: margin erodes steadily as discount depth increases, suggesting current discount thresholds (particularly 40%+) should be reviewed rather than used as a default lever for driving volume. Most notably, five sub-categories, Machines, Bookcases, Tables, Appliances, and Binders, are losing money on average despite meaningful sales volume, making them the clearest, most actionable finding in this analysis: each is a candidate for repricing, renegotiated supplier costs, or discontinuation.
